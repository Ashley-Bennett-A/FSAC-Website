Coastal Dive Sites

RHIB dives are very popular because of the fun getting to the sites. the North Wales coast offers many accessible wrecks, many not yet identified. FSAC has a data base of over 70 wrecks it uses. There are many more. The list below shows some of the most popular:

  • Missouri - A white star cargo ship of 5,146 tons that sank off Porth dafarch in 1886.
  • Penrhos - A steamship of 187 tons hit a mine off Colwyn Bay in 1942
  • Torch - A steamship of 274 tons foundered after a collision off the great Orme.
  • Lelia - A paddle steamer intended to be used as a confederate gun runner. It sank in 1865 in Liverpool Bay.
  • Calcium - A cargo ship of 613 tonnes sank in 1940 off Rhyl after hitting a mine.
  • Dublin - A steam ferry of 599 tons sank off Rhyl after a collision in 1888.
  • Penstone - A cargo ship of 267 tons sank close to the Dublin in 1948 after a collision.
  • Mona - A steam cargo ship of 207 tons foundered in 1916 to the east of Puffin island.

  • Segontium - This is a mussel dredger of 192 tons sank in 1984, cause unknown.
  • Norman Court - A famous tea clipper at one time faster than the Cutty Sark. Sank in 1883 off Cymran Bay.
  • Asmund - A steamship of 4,730 tons that ran ashore then sank in Holyhead harbour.
  • Mermaid - A wooden hulled steamship of 88 tons sank in 1894 off the Great Orme.
  • Cambank - A cargo steamship of 3112 tons sank by a torpedo in 1915 off Dulas Bay Anglesey.
  • Dakota - A steamship of 4,331 tons that ran ashore off Amlwch in 1877.
  • Alarm - A lightship of 224 tons sank off Rhyl (near the wind farm) after a collision in 1911.
  • Gwynfaen - A steam cargo ship of 279 tons that ran aground in 1904 just off Trwyn Porth Dinllaen.
